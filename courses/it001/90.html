Appendix

Excursion: Re. chapter 7: Use of the words "expression" and "term"

Be aware that the understanding on expressions in this course is deviating from the understanding in computer science. In computer science, an expression is

a combination of explicit values, constants, variables, operators, and functions that are interpreted according to the particular rules of precedence and of association for a particular programming language, which computes and then produces (returns, in a stateful environment) another value. (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(programming)).

The official definition of an "expression" also resembles the definition of "expression" in mathematics which is

a finite combination of symbols that is well-formed according to rules that depend on the context. (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(mathematics))

Also, our understanding of the word "term" is a little bit different from the understanding in mathematics. Unfortunately, the mathematical understanding of "term" does not have much practical relevance in programming. In mathematics, a "term" is

parts of an expression or series separated by + or - signs, or the parts of a sequence separated by commas. (http://www.mathwords.com/t/term.htm)

In this course, when we talk of an "expression" we really mean an assignment, and when we talk of a "term" then we mean the construct to the right hand side of the = sign. When talking about the more mathematical side of programming we purposely use "expression", and not "assignment". "Expression" has a closer connotation with "a mathematical operation" whilst "assignment" is more related to "an instruction to be executed". The area of expressions deals more with the mathematical concepts of programming than with instructions and what they do.

Our definition vs Official definition
 

As per official definitions, "expression" and "term" isn't easy to distinguish. In mathematics, a term is part of an expression, but also is an expression in itself. It's not so problematic in mathematics, because there we use equations, not just assignments. In an equation it's legal to have operators and terms on both sides of the = sign: 

    3b + 1 = b + 15
<=> b = 7

However, in programming we don't have equations, only assignments! And it's important to distinguish between the entire assignment (here called "expression") and the right hand side of the assignment (here called "term"). This distinction is important, because we strongly discourage the use of expressions where terms should be used. This practice can lead to side effects and produce hard-to-maintain code. Here are a few examples:

  • Chained expressions - avoid these, as they have hidden side effects, especially when using them as part of a variable declaration: 

    var a = b = 5;

    Whilst this is a great shorthand syntax, it creates implicit global variables. Consider this: 

    var bar = 'funky';
var baz = 'disco';

(function helloworld() {
    var foo = bar = baz = 'local';
    console.log(foo); // local
    console.log(bar); // local
    console.log(baz); // local
}());

console.log(bar);// local, I thought it would be funky
console.log(baz);// local, I thought it would be disco

    We discourage chained expressions. They should be split up in separate expressions with just terms on the right hand side.

    Example courtesy of this article

  • Expressions instead of terms - Leading to some monstrocities such as these: 

    a = 3 + (b = 5);

    b = (a > 5)  ?  c = 3; c = 4;

    if (b = 4) 
{
    /* do something */ 
};

    These are notoriously bad, because they have side effects and make the code harder to understand - potentially leading to buggy and unmaintainable code. Therefore we simply cut through these issues by making a distinction between terms and expressions and saying: "Don't use expressions where terms should be used!" This gives students just that bit better foundation to write safer and more maintainable code!

In summa...

Whilst it's unfortunate that our understanding of "expression" and "term" in this course deviates from the official definitions, we hope that the redefinitions help the student to better know how to use terms and expressions.

We hope that there will be the positive net effect of a better understanding of the concepts behind expressions and terms in programming, helping the student to write better (i.e. less error prone and more maintainable) code.
